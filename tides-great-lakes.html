Great Lakes Water Levels: What Actually Moves the Water

If you're used to ocean tide charts, the Great Lakes take some recalibrating. There's a real, measurable tide here — but it's not what moves the water day to day.

The tide is real but tiny

NOAA has measured a genuine astronomical tide on the Great Lakes, but it amounts to roughly an inch or two of range — utterly overshadowed by other forces. We cover the physics of why in our do lakes have tides guide; the short version is that a lake simply isn't large enough for the moon's pull to create a noticeable bulge the way it does across an ocean basin.

Seiches: the real short-term mover

What actually swings Great Lakes water levels by feet in a matter of hours is a seiche — a standing wave that sloshes across a lake basin when strong, sustained wind or a fast-moving pressure system pushes water toward one shore. Lake Erie, being long, shallow, and oriented along its prevailing wind direction, is especially prone to large seiches; a strong wind event can raise water several feet on one shore while lowering it on the opposite shore at the same time.

Seasonal and multi-year swings

On top of daily wind effects, the lakes rise and fall over weeks and years based on precipitation, snowmelt, and evaporation — a slow cycle tracked separately by NOAA and the Army Corps of Engineers, and unrelated to both tide and seiche.

What this means on the water

Great Lakes boaters and anglers watch wind forecasts and real-time water level gauges far more closely than any tide table — see our guide to NOAA station types for how to find a real-time water-level station near you. Ice season also shuts down most water level activity across the northern lakes for several months, a factor that has no equivalent on the ocean coasts.
