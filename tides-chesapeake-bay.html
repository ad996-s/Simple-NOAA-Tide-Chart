Chesapeake Bay Tides

The Chesapeake is the largest estuary in the United States, and its sheer size means the tide doesn't move through it all at once — high tide can arrive hours apart at different points on the same bay.

Tide timing shifts as you move up the bay

Water entering the bay's mouth near Norfolk and Virginia Beach takes real time to work its way up toward Baltimore and the Susquehanna Flats, so the tide at the northern end of the bay lags well behind the tide at the mouth — this is exactly why picking the right nearby station matters so much here specifically. See our guide to NOAA stations for how Simple Tides handles this.

A shallow bay where wind competes with tide

Much of the Chesapeake is quite shallow relative to its width, especially in the upper bay, which means sustained wind can push water around almost as forcefully as the tide itself — similar to the dynamic covered in our Gulf Coast tides guide, just in an estuary rather than the open Gulf.

The heart of blue crab country

The Chesapeake is the single most significant blue crab fishery in the country, and tide timing genuinely matters for it — see our crabbing and tides guide for how crabbers use the incoming tide to place traps. The bay's countless creeks, marshes, and grass flats also make it prime water for tide-driven inshore fishing.

Storm season

Hurricane remnants and strong nor'easters can push meaningful storm surge up the bay, and the funnel shape of the upper Chesapeake can amplify that surge well beyond what the open Atlantic coast sees from the same storm.
